Design

The case for removing everything

2026-06-20

Minimalism is not a lack of something. It is the perfect amount of something. Every designer eventually confronts the question: what stays, and what goes?

The answer is uncomfortable. Most things go.

Start with subtraction

Before adding anything to a layout, ask what already exists that could be removed. A border that defines space can often be replaced by whitespace alone. A label that explains a field can often be replaced by a clear placeholder.

Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away.

This applies to color, typefaces, and motion just as much as it applies to layout elements. Each addition must earn its place.

Hierarchy without decoration

Strong hierarchy does not require color. It requires contrast — in size, weight, and spacing. Consider what you can communicate with:

  • Size — large text commands attention
  • Weight — bold separates signal from noise
  • Space — distance implies relationship

When those three tools are used with discipline, color becomes optional. Use font-weight: 700 for headings, font-weight: 400 for body. Nothing in between.

What remains

After removing everything unnecessary, what remains is content. The reader arrives at the words without friction. That is the goal. Read more about this in the full essay. 

:root {
  --color-bg:   #ffffff;
  --color-text: #000000;

  --font-sans: "Castledown", Georgia, serif;
  --weight-regular: 400;
  --weight-bold:    700;
}

These are the only variables the design needs. Every decision flows from them. Nothing is added without a reason.
